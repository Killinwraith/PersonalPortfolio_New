If you've looked at ten developer portfolios in a row, you've looked at one. The formula is so consistent it's almost ritual: a hero with the person's name and a one-line tagline; a grid of projects with screenshots; a list of jobs in reverse chronological order; a contact section with the same five icons.

There's nothing *wrong* with the formula. It loads quickly, it's recruiter-legible, it does the job. The problem is that the job it does is *not actually the job you want it to do*. The job you want is for someone to come away with a feel for who you are. The job it does is communicate that you are a person who can build a website.

## the substitution

The mistake is treating "portfolio" as a fixed genre — like a résumé — instead of as a personal site that happens to have your work on it. A résumé has rules because everyone is reading it for the same thing. A personal site has no rules because anyone landing on it might be looking for anything.

The interesting move is to *invert* the assumption. Instead of asking "what should a developer portfolio have?", ask "if I had to make a site that captures me, what would it be?". For some people the answer is still a clean grid of projects. For most people it isn't.

## what helps

A few things that consistently improve a personal site, ranked by how much they pay back relative to the effort:

1. **Replace the tagline with a sentence**. Taglines are a meta-promise to summarize you; a sentence actually does it.
2. **Show one thing in depth instead of ten things in passing**. People remember one well-told story; they don't remember a grid.
3. **Let a non-code interest into the room**. Travel, fashion, food, music — whatever you actually care about. It contextualizes the code.
4. **Use real photography, not stock**. Your photos of your trip are infinitely more interesting than the polished landscape stock photo.
5. **Write in your voice**. If you wouldn't say it out loud, don't put it on the site.

None of this requires being a designer. It just requires being willing to make something that doesn't look like everyone else's.
