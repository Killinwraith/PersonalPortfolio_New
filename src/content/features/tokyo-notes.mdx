The first morning I left the hotel before anyone in the office expected me to be at a desk. Shimokitazawa was a twenty-minute ride and a different city — older, smaller, leaning. I bought a Carhartt double-knee at a place that didn't have a name on the door and a coffee at a place that did.

## the side streets

The argument for staying in Tokyo three more days than the trip required is that *every* block has a version of itself you'd want to know better. The argument for leaving is that this is true everywhere and you can't keep doing it.

I split the difference. Two short days became one long one, structured like:

- Daikanyama in the morning, the Tsutaya store and walking.
- Lunch in Ebisu, soba, alone, with my phone in my pocket because the room demanded it.
- An afternoon in Shinjuku that became evening without my noticing.

## the bar

Golden Gai is six rows of bars that fit six people each. The one I ended up in fit four. I don't want to write its name down here because it would be a small betrayal — the kind of place that earns its reputation by *not* trending. The bartender played records and didn't talk to me until my second drink, at which point he asked what I do. I said I write software. He nodded like this was a reasonable thing for someone in his bar to do, which I appreciated.

## what I'd do differently

Skip Shibuya Crossing. Skip the observation decks. Eat at the convenience stores without apologizing for it. Bring less.
